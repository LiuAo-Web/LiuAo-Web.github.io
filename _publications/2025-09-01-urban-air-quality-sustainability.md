**Abstract:** Analyzing the impact mechanism of urban transportation on air quality is integral to developing sustainable transportation systems. However, previous studies often lack the spatial precision and coverage necessary to capture their complex relationship accurately. This study, therefore, examines 16 major Metropolitan Statistical Areas across the United States, leveraging large-scale, high-resolution data on air quality, transportation, land use, population, meteorology, and industrial activity. By integrating multi-source datasets, this study employs an interpretable machine learning model with hyperparameters optimized by the Bayesian optimization algorithm to uncover the nonlinear relationship between transportation and air quality. Our findings indicate that transportation systems strongly correlate with NO2 and PM2.5 concentrations, contributing 14.9% and 4.7%, respectively, to observed variations. Traffic volume exerts the greatest influence on NO2 due to its impact on energy consumption and vehicle emissions, while congestion plays a pivotal role in PM2.5 concentrations by affecting idling times and repeated acceleration or deceleration. Notably, this congestion effect intensifies initially but diminishes beyond a certain threshold. Furthermore, applying the proposed modeling framework to evaluate fuel tax policies suggests that raising fuel taxes can effectively reduce NO2 by 1.0%-4.7% and PM2.5 by 0.8%-1.4%, translating into health and economic benefits of approximately 5.8-22.8 billion dollars.
